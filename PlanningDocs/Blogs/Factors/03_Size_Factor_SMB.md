# Small Caps vs. Large Caps: More Than Just Company Size

## The Size Premium: Small Companies, Bigger Returns?

In 1981, Rolf Banz discovered something that challenged everything finance professors taught: **small stocks beat large stocks.**

Looking at NYSE data from 1926 to 1975, Banz found that the smallest companies outperformed the largest by roughly 3-5% annually. This wasn't supposed to happen. According to the efficient market hypothesis, you can't get higher returns without taking more risk. So either small stocks were riskier than anyone thought, or markets weren't as efficient as believed.

Thus began one of the most studied phenomena in finance: the **size factor**, often called **SMB** (Small Minus Big).

---

## What Is the Size Factor?

The size factor captures the historical tendency of small-capitalization stocks to outperform large-capitalization stocks over long periods.

**How it's measured:**
- Rank all stocks by market capitalization
- Split into small (bottom 50%) and big (top 50%)
- **SMB = Return of small stocks - Return of big stocks**

When SMB is positive, small caps are outperforming. When negative, large caps are winning.

**What it means for your portfolio:**
- **Positive size exposure**: Your portfolio tilts toward smaller companies
- **Negative size exposure**: Your portfolio tilts toward larger companies
- **Neutral (zero)**: Balanced between small and large, similar to market weights

---

## The Evidence: Does Small Really Beat Big?

Let's look at the numbers:

### Long-Term Data (1926-2023)

| Category | Annualized Return | Best Year | Worst Year |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|------------|
| Small Cap (Decile 10) | 11.8% | +142.9% (1933) | -58.0% (1937) |
| Large Cap (Decile 1) | 9.4% | +54.0% (1933) | -43.3% (1931) |
| Size Premium | ~2.4% | | |

Over nearly a century, small stocks returned roughly 2-3% more annually than large stocks. Compounded over decades, that's enormous.

**$10,000 invested in 1926:**
- Large caps → ~$11 million today
- Small caps → ~$50 million today

### But Here's the Catch...

The size premium hasn't been consistent:

- **1926-1980**: Strong size premium (~5% annually)
- **1980-2000**: Weak/negative size premium
- **2000-2010**: Strong small cap outperformance
- **2010-2020**: Large caps dominated (think FAANG)
- **2020-2023**: Mixed results, large still winning

The size factor goes through long periods of underperformance. The 2010s were particularly brutal for small cap investors, as mega-cap tech stocks dominated returns.

## Why Do Small Stocks Have Higher Returns?

There are two schools of thought:

### The Risk-Based Explanation

Small companies are genuinely riskier:

1. **Less diversified businesses**: A small company might have one product, one factory, one market
2. **Weaker balance sheets**: Less access to capital markets during stress
3. **Higher bankruptcy risk**: Small companies fail more often
4. **Lower liquidity**: Harder to buy/sell without moving prices
5. **Less analyst coverage**: More uncertainty about fair value

Investors demand compensation for these risks → higher expected returns.

### The Behavioral Explanation

Market inefficiencies favor small stocks:

1. **Neglect effect**: Institutions can't buy small stocks (too small to matter for a $100B fund)
2. **Information asymmetry**: Less public information means more opportunity for informed investors
3. **Lottery preferences**: Investors overpay for "exciting" large caps, underpay for boring small caps

Both explanations probably contain truth. Small stocks are riskier AND somewhat neglected.

## When Does Size Work (And When Doesn't It)?

### Size Factor Performance by Environment

| Environment | Size Factor Typically |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Economic expansion | Positive (small caps thrive) |
| Recession | Negative (flight to quality) |
| Low interest rates | Negative (benefits large caps with easy financing) |
| Rising rates | Positive (small cap value thrives) |
| Risk-on markets | Positive |
| Risk-off markets | Negative |

### The 2010s: When Size Failed

The 2010s were the worst decade for the size factor since the 1930s. Why?

1. **Zero interest rates**: Large caps could borrow cheaply to buy back shares, boost earnings
2. **Winner-take-all dynamics**: Network effects concentrated gains in largest tech companies
3. **Passive investing surge**: Index funds weight by market cap, pushing money into largest stocks
4. **Globalization benefits**: Large multinationals could access global growth; small caps couldn't

Many investors abandoned small caps entirely. That's often when a factor is about to turn.

## What Size Exposure Looks Like In Practice

### Example Portfolio Analysis

Let's analyze three investors:

**Maria - The Index Investor**
- 100% S&P 500 index fund
- **Size exposure: Slightly negative**
- Why? S&P 500 is market-cap weighted, dominated by mega caps

**James - The Stock Picker**
- Owns Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Nvidia, Tesla
- **Size exposure: Strongly negative**
- Why? All mega-caps, no small company exposure at all

**Rachel - The Diversified Investor**
- 60% Total market fund
- 20% Small cap value fund
- 20% International developed
- **Size exposure: Slightly positive**
- Why? Small cap tilt offsets market-cap weighting

Most investors unknowingly have **negative size exposure**—they're betting on large caps without realizing it.

## The Small Cap Value Sweet Spot

Here's a secret the quant community has known for decades: **small cap VALUE is the real magic.**

When you combine two factors—size and value—the returns amplify:

| Category | Annualized Return (1926-2023) |
|----------|------------------------------|
| Large Growth | 9.5% |
| Large Value | 11.0% |
| Small Growth | 9.0% |
| Small Value | 13.5% |

Small value stocks—cheap small companies—have been the best-performing segment of the market over the long term. By a lot.

But they're also the most volatile and painful to own during drawdowns. There's no free lunch.

## Hidden Size Bets You Might Be Making

Many investors have unintended size exposure:

### Accidental Large Cap Bets
- Target-date funds (heavily large cap)
- Most actively managed "growth" funds
- Quality-focused strategies (quality correlates with size)
- Dividend strategies (large caps pay more dividends)

### Accidental Small Cap Bets
- Sector ETFs (equal-weighted within sector)
- "Dogs of the Dow" type strategies
- Deep value strategies
- Momentum strategies (often finds small caps)

**The key is awareness.** Whether you tilt toward large or small caps should be intentional, not accidental.

## How to Think About Size In Your Portfolio

### Questions to Ask Yourself

1. **What's my time horizon?**
   - Longer horizon → can tolerate small cap volatility
   - Shorter horizon → prefer large cap stability

2. **What's my risk tolerance?**
   - Small caps can drop 50%+ in bad years
   - Can you hold through that without selling?

3. **What are my other factor exposures?**
   - Already have high beta? Adding small caps increases risk
   - Already value-tilted? Small value doubles down

4. **What's my view on the next decade?**
   - Believe large caps will keep winning? Stay large
   - Think small cap is due for reversion? Add exposure

### A Reasonable Approach

For most investors, a moderate small cap tilt makes sense:

- **10-20% allocation to small cap** (via dedicated fund)
- **Prefer small cap value** over small cap growth
- **Rebalance regularly** to maintain target allocation
- **Expect long periods of underperformance**—it's the price of higher expected returns
